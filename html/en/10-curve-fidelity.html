Chapter 10 · Quality

Curve Fidelity & Continuity

The single most visible quality difference between a good generator and a bad one lies in how it handles curves. Quality output must satisfy three things at once: curves read as curves (no faceting), joins read as joins (no breaks), and the result stays faithful to the design within a controlled tolerance. This chapter collects every technique that protects those three properties, from curve flattening through chain assembly to the final emitted motion.

Smooth

Curves read as curves, not jagged polygons. No visible flat facets, no aliasing ("không răng cưa").

Continuous

No gaps, no accidental breaks between segments that should be one flowing stroke ("không đứt đoạn").

Faithful

The toolpath tracks the design within a controlled tolerance - no missing tips ("mất nét"), no bloating or distortion.

Applies to every chain source

The principles here apply to all chain-generating sources: fill, outline, text, pattern. Wherever a chain comes from, all three properties above must be protected uniformly.

1. Where jaggedness and breaks come from

Before fixing anything, classify the symptom and trace it back to its root cause. Nearly every curve-quality defect falls into one of seven groups below, each with a specific fix in the sections that follow.

2. Flattening quality (the anti-facet foundation)

A curve turns into line segments exactly once, at the flattening step. That is where smoothness is won or lost - poor flattening cannot be rescued by anything downstream.

2.1 Control tolerance in world units

A curve flattened with a tolerance fixed in path units facets badly once viewed at mm scale. Make the effective tolerance a real-world distance sitting below the tool's mark width. This is the scale-up trick (see Ch. 2 §1): multiply the path by a factor k (e.g. k ≈ 25), flatten, then scale back down. The result is an effective tolerance ≈ libraryTolerance / k ≈ 0.02 mm. At that size, facets are no longer physically resolvable, so the curve looks perfectly smooth.

flatness test

A Bézier span is considered "flat enough" once the deviation of its control points from the chord joining its two endpoints falls below tolerance. If not, subdivide the span (de Casteljau) and re-test each half.

2.2 Prefer curvature-adaptive subdivision

The best flatteners place more points where curvature is high and fewer on near-straight spans. This is curvature-adaptive subdivision: recursively subdividing a Bézier span (recursive de Casteljau subdivision) until the control-point deviation falls below tolerance. This approach gives a smooth curve and compact output at the same time. A uniform-step flattener wastes points on straight stretches while still being able to facet sharp corners - adaptive subdivision is strictly better.

2.3 The tolerance trade-off

# Two directions of trade-off
SMALLER tolerance → smoother curve, MORE points → bigger output, slower stream
LARGER  tolerance → fewer points                → faceting

Pick the tolerance as "just below the tool's mark width": ≈ 0.02 mm as the fine default (Ch. 2 §1), and ≈ 0.05 mm as a coarser tier, acceptable only for boundaries used in non-output decisions (metrics, tile bounds, inside/outside tests).

The coarse 0.05 mm tier is never used for an emitted vertex

Never use the coarse tolerance for a boundary whose intersections become the endpoints of an emitted fill/pattern stroke, or that must align with an outline. Doing so reproduces exactly the misaligned seam this section warns against. Both figures are estimates tied to tool resolution, not hard constants. Do not shrink the tolerance arbitrarily either: beyond the tool's resolution you only add streaming load (Ch. 11) with zero visible gain.

2.4 Keep a single source of truth

Flatten every curve through one routine with one tolerance policy (a fine tolerance for anything the eye follows). If different features flatten at different tolerances, a fill line and the outline it meets won't align, producing visible seams.

One routine, one policy

The "single source of truth" principle defends against both failures at once: uneven faceting and mismatched seams between features. Don't let each module pick its own tolerance.

3. Continuity: no gaps, no accidental breaks

"Continuity" has two levels, borrowed from CAD's geometric continuity classes (written G⁰/G¹ here):

G⁰/G¹ is NOT the G-code commands G0/G1

Don't confuse the two. G⁰/G¹ here are geometric continuity classes (positional / tangential continuity). G0/G1 in Ch. 8 are G-code motion commands (rapid move / linear interpolation). They are entirely unrelated - the matching characters are pure coincidence.

G⁰ (positional)
Two consecutive segments share exactly one endpoint - no gap. But the direction of travel through that shared point can change abruptly (still a visible kink).
G¹ (tangential)
Beyond sharing the point, the two segments also share a direction at the join - no visible kink. The stroke flows smoothly through the join.

3.1 Weld coincident endpoints (kill gaps)

Whenever two segments/chains are supposed to meet, force their shared point to be identical, not merely close. Snap endpoints within an epsilon to a common value (this is endpoint welding / vertex snapping). Never rely on two independently computed points being bit-equal. A sub-tolerance gap turns into a visible pen-up + travel + pen-down move - the classic "đứt đoạn" (break) bug.

3.2 Close loops explicitly

A closed subpath must have first == last within ε. If flattening or an intermediate operation left them slightly apart, re-append the first vertex to close the loop. Test closure with a squared-distance epsilon over at least 3-4 points (Ch. 2 §2). An unclosed loop leaves a small gap right at the start/end join - exactly the defect described in §3.1.

3.3 Don't fragment contiguous runs

When clipping fill/pattern lines to a region (Ch. 2 §6), merge adjacent inside sub-segments into one chain rather than emitting each tiny piece separately. Only a genuine exit-and-re-enter should ever break a chain. Emitting per-piece produces hundreds of micro-chains and a stuttering, lift-heavy path.

3.4 The boundary-probe nudge (a subtle break bug)

A connector that runs along a region's edge (e.g. a boustrophedon turn) has its midpoint sitting exactly on the boundary, where a strict contains() returns false and the connector gets wrongly rejected - splitting one smooth fill into many separate strokes. Fix: before the inside/outside test, nudge the probe point a hair (≈ 0.25 · spacing) toward the region's bounding-box center (a cheap approximate centroid). This preserves valid edge connectors and keeps the fill continuous.

Caveat: concave or holed regions

The bounding-box center is only a valid interior reference for convex-ish regions. On concave or holed regions it can lie outside the material; in that case the nudge merely biases the test toward rejecting the connector - which is safe, since it never validates a move that crosses a gap. See Ch. 3 §3.3 for the local-test fallback.

3.5 Join fragments into flowing strokes

Skeleton tracing and per-segment extraction naturally produce many fragments. Rejoin them at shared endpoints following the straightest continuation rule (max direction dot-product, turn < 90°), so a scribble of pieces becomes a handful of flowing strokes (Ch. 5 §3.5). The result is G¹-continuous runs with far fewer pen lifts.

4. Smoothing generated polylines (kill wobble & staircase)

Some chain sources (raster skeletons, traced bitmaps) are born jagged. Apply these three steps in exactly this order: denoise → simplify → round.

1 · Moving average [1,2,1]/4 → 2 · Douglas–Peucker → 3 · Chaikin
1 · Moving-average low-pass
Moving-average low-pass ([1,2,1]/4, a few passes) - removes ±1-sample staircase wobble. Pin the endpoints of an open path; wrap around a closed loop.
2 · Simplify
Douglas–Peucker at a small tolerance - drops redundant collinear points without changing the shape (fewer points = faster stream, no quality loss).
3 · Round corners
Chaikin corner-cutting (1-2 iterations) - rounds remaining corners into a smooth curve.
Caution: over-smoothing

Smoothing too aggressively will shrink the shape and round off intended sharp corners. Keep tolerances small and iteration counts low. When a shape has intended sharp corners, a corner-preserving smoother (angle-thresholded) beats blanket Chaikin applied everywhere.

5. Consistent point density

Very uneven segment lengths make a controller's motion planner surge and stall (one long move followed by a burst of tiny ones), which looks rough even though the geometry itself is smooth. Two mitigations:

  • Adaptive flattening already spaces points by curvature - which is exactly what's needed.
  • Optionally resample a chain to a maximum segment length on long straight runs, so the feed rate stays even. Balance this against output size.

6. Arc fitting (optional, advanced)

Instead of many short line segments, fit circular arcs to smooth spans and emit native arc commands (G-code G2/G3; HPGL AA/AR/CI).

Cost: arc fitting (e.g. biarc fitting onto Bézier spans) is more complex, and not every controller supports arcs well. Use line flattening as the robust default, and arc fitting as an optimisation where the target dialect supports it. Keywords: biarc/arc fitting, G2/G3 arc interpolation.

7. Avoid integer-rounding aliasing at emission

Some dialects use integer device units (e.g. HPGL: 40 units/mm). Rounding each point independently can reintroduce a fine staircase on shallow diagonals. Mitigate with two rules:

  • Emit at the finest device unit available (40 units/mm ≈ 0.025 mm steps, below tool resolution and therefore acceptable).
  • Keep all geometry in floating-point mm and round only at the final format() step (Ch. 8 §4) - never round mid-pipeline and keep computing on the rounded values (errors compound into visible drift/gaps).
Golden rule: compute in float, round only at emission

Every computation in the pipeline stays in floating-point mm. Rounding to device units is the last step, done exactly once, right at format(). This is the surest way to keep rounding error from accumulating into a distorted shape.

8. Checklist (AI protecting curve quality)

Quality checklist
  • Flatten with a world-space tolerance ≈ 0.02 mm (scale-up trick or adaptive subdivision); one routine, one policy.
  • Prefer curvature-adaptive subdivision over uniform stepping.
  • Weld coincident endpoints; close loops explicitly (ε test).
  • Merge contiguous clipped runs; don't emit per-piece micro-chains.
  • Nudge boundary-probe points toward the bounding-box center so edge connectors survive.
  • Join fragments by straightest continuation (G¹) into flowing strokes.
  • Smooth → simplify → round, with small tolerances, respecting corners.
  • Round to device units only at emission; compute in float mm throughout.
  • Consider arc fitting where the target dialect supports G2/G3.
